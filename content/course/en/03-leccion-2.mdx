## The Four Types of Motion

When two voices move from one chord to the next, their relationship can be one of four types:

| Type | Description | Quality |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Contrary** | Voices move in opposite directions | ⭐ Preferred |
| **Oblique** | One voice moves, the other stays | ✅ Good |
| **Similar** | Both voices move in the same direction but by different intervals | ⚠️ Allowed with care |
| **Parallel** | Both voices move in the same direction by the same interval | ❌ Restricted |

---

## Contrary Motion: Why It's Preferred

Contrary motion produces the greatest **voice independence**. When the soprano rises, the bass falls — each voice "does its own thing."

This independence is what makes the chorale sound like 4 real voices rather than a single block chord moving around.

> 💡 Actively seek contrary motion, especially between the outer voices (soprano and bass). The inner voices will follow more naturally.

---

## Voice Overlap

**Voice overlap** occurs when a voice, in the next chord, rises above the note the upper voice had in the previous chord.

**Example:**
- Measure 1: Alto = B3
- Measure 2: Tenor = C4

The tenor in measure 2 (C4) is higher than the alto in measure 1 (B3). That is voice overlap. ⚠️

It is not as serious as voice crossing, but it makes the chorale sound clumsy.

---

## Leading the Inner Voices

Alto and Tenor are the "forgotten" voices for beginners. They tend to jump wherever needed to complete the chord. That's wrong.

Rules for inner voices:

1. **Move them as little as possible.** If a note of the next chord is already in the voice, stay on it.
2. **Prefer stepwise motion** (seconds) when you must move.
3. **Avoid leaps larger than a fifth** in inner voices.

---

## Hidden Fifths in Outer Voices

You already know about parallel fifths. There's a subtler variant: **hidden fifths**.

They occur when soprano and bass reach a perfect fifth by **similar motion** (both going up or both going down).

The problem is that even though they're not technically "parallel," the ear perceives the harshness of the fifth interval.

**Rule:** Avoid having soprano and bass reach a perfect fifth by direct (similar) motion. If necessary, make sure at least the soprano arrives by stepwise motion.
