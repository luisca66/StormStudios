## The Four-Voice Chorale

**Four-voice chorale writing** (SATB: Soprano, Alto, Tenor, Bass) is the cornerstone of traditional harmony. 90% of the rules you will learn in this course come from classical chorale practice — from Bach to Shostakovich.

Before writing a single note, you need to know three fundamental things:

1. The **vocal ranges** of each voice
2. How to **arrange** the 4 voices on the staff
3. The **two basic rules** you must never break

---

## SATB Vocal Ranges

Each voice has a natural range that defines the notes it can comfortably sing:

| Voice | Comfortable range | Extreme notes |
|-------|------------------|---------------|
| **Soprano** | D4 – A5 | C4 – G5 |
| **Alto** | G3 – D5 | E3 – F5 |
| **Tenor** | C3 – G4 | B2 – A4 |
| **Bass** | E2 – B3 | C2 – E4 |

> ⚠️ Writing notes outside of range is not "incorrect" in a harmonic sense, but it does make the chorale impossible to sing. The Virtual Teacher will flag these notes as errors.

---

## Staff Layout

In standard SATB chorale:

- **Soprano and Alto** are written on the **upper staff** (treble clef)
  - Soprano: stems up ↑
  - Alto: stems down ↓
- **Tenor and Bass** are written on the **lower staff** (bass clef)
  - Tenor: stems up ↑
  - Bass: stems down ↓

---

## Rule 1: No Voice Crossing

Voices must stay in order of pitch:

**Soprano > Alto > Tenor > Bass**

If the Alto rises so high that it is above the Soprano, that is a **voice crossing** — one of the most common beginner errors.

---

## Rule 2: No Parallel Fifths or Octaves

When **any two voices** move from one chord to the next in **parallel perfect fifths** or **parallel octaves**, the result is a "hollow" sound that classical composers systematically avoided.

**Parallel fifths:**
- Measure 1: Soprano = G4, Alto = C4 (interval: perfect fifth)
- Measure 2: Soprano = A4, Alto = D4 (perfect fifth again, both voices moved up a step)
- This is parallel fifths! ❌

**Parallel octaves:**
- Two voices forming an octave, both moving the same interval in the same direction. ❌

> 💡 The rule against parallel fifths and octaves stems from **voice independence**: we want each voice to sound like an independent melodic line. Parallel fifths and octaves "fuse" two voices into one.
