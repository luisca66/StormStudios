## The minor key

Every major scale has a **relative minor** that shares its exact key signature: the same notes, but a different **tonic**. The relative minor begins on the **6th degree** of the major.

> C major and **A minor** use the same notes (all white keys). What changes is the center of gravity: now everything revolves around **A**.

That is why we go through the minor keys in the **same order** as the major ones, but through their relatives.

## The three forms of the minor scale

The minor scale is not just one: it has **three forms**, and all of them live side by side in tonal music. Let's use **A minor** as our example.

| Form | Notes | What changes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Natural** | A B C D E F G A | The key signature as is. This is the Aeolian mode. |
| **Harmonic** | A B C D E F **G♯** A | The **7th** (leading tone) is raised so it "pulls" toward the tonic. |
| **Melodic** ↑ | A B C D E **F♯ G♯** A | Both **6th and 7th** rise to smooth out the leap. |
| **Melodic** ↓ | A G F E D C B A | Coming down it reverts to **natural**. |

Two key ideas:

- The **harmonic** form creates an **augmented 2nd** between the 6th and 7th (F → G♯). That leap is its signature sound.
- The **melodic** form removes that augmented 2nd on the way up (it also raises the 6th), but **descending** it no longer needs the pull toward the tonic, so it **comes down as the natural** scale.

## The order by relatives

We go through all 15 minor keys following the major circle of fifths, by relatives:

> **Sharps:** A · E · B · F♯ · C♯ · G♯ · D♯ · A♯ minor
>
> **Flats:** D · G · C · F · B♭ · E♭ · A♭ minor
